In 1905 Einstein published a paper that began not with mathematics, but with a thought experiment.
Imagine riding alongside a beam of light — what would you see? Classical physics had no good answer.
Einstein concluded that the question itself was wrong: **nothing can travel with or alongside light**,
because the speed of light is the same for every observer, no matter how fast they are moving.

This single postulate — the constancy of *c* — forces something uncomfortable: time itself cannot
be universal. Two observers in relative motion do not agree on how much time has passed.

## The Light Clock

Place two mirrors facing each other, separated by a distance $d$. A photon bouncing between them
ticks once per round trip. For an observer at rest with the clock:

<div class="equation-block">
  <div class="equation-block__label">Rest frame</div>

  $$\Delta t_0 = \frac{2d}{c}$$

</div>

Now watch the same clock from a platform as it moves past at speed $v$. The photon traces a diagonal
path — longer than before — yet its speed has not changed. The tick must therefore take longer.

  <div class="widget__label">Interactive — Light Clock</div>
  <div id="widget-light-clock"></div>
</div>

By Pythagoras, solving for $\Delta t$ gives the central result:

<div class="equation-block">
  <div class="equation-block__label">Time dilation</div>

  $$\Delta t = \gamma\, \Delta t_0 \qquad \text{where} \qquad \gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}}$$

</div>

<div class="callout callout--insight">
  <div class="callout__label">Key insight</div>

  The factor $\gamma$ is always $\geq 1$. A moving clock always ticks slower than a stationary one.
  At 87% of the speed of light, $\gamma = 2$: the moving clock ticks at half the rate.

</div>

## Derivation Step by Step

<div class="derivation">
  <div class="derivation__label">Derivation — Time Dilation from the Light Clock</div>

  <div class="derivation__step">
    <div class="derivation__step-num">1</div>
    <div class="derivation__step-body">

    In the rest frame, the photon travels a distance $2d$ per tick:
    $$\Delta t_0 = \frac{2d}{c}$$

    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="derivation__step">
    <div class="derivation__step-num">2</div>
    <div class="derivation__step-body">

    In the moving frame, the photon traces a diagonal. By Pythagoras, the total path per tick
    is $2\sqrt{d^2 + (v\,\Delta t/2)^2}$.

    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="derivation__step">
    <div class="derivation__step-num">3</div>
    <div class="derivation__step-body">

    Since $c$ is the same in all frames:
    $$c\,\Delta t = 2\sqrt{d^2 + \left(\frac{v\,\Delta t}{2}\right)^2}$$

    </div>
  </div>

  <div class="derivation__step">
    <div class="derivation__step-num">4</div>
    <div class="derivation__step-body">

    Solving for $\Delta t$:
    $$\Delta t = \frac{\Delta t_0}{\sqrt{1 - v^2/c^2}} = \gamma\,\Delta t_0$$

    </div>
  </div>

</div>

## Real-World Evidence

Muons created in the upper atmosphere at 99.5% of the speed of light should decay long before
reaching Earth. Yet we detect them in abundance at sea level. Their internal clocks run slow.
With $\gamma \approx 10$, they survive the journey.

<div class="callout callout--warning">
  <div class="callout__label">Common misconception</div>

  Time dilation is *not* about clocks being inaccurate. It is about the actual rate at which
  physical processes unfold — decay, aging, chemical reactions. The muon genuinely lives longer.

</div>

<div class="callout callout--insight">
  <div class="callout__label">The Language of Physicists</div>

  Physicists distinguish **proper time** $\tau$ (measured by a clock travelling with the object)
  from **coordinate time** $t$ (measured in an external frame). Time dilation: $t = \gamma\tau$.
  When $v \to 0$, $\gamma \to 1$ — Newtonian time is recovered as a special case.

</div>

## Practice

<div class="exercise">
  <div class="exercise__label">Exercise 1</div>

  A muon travels at $v = 0.995c$. Its rest-frame lifetime is $\tau = 2.2\,\mu\text{s}$.
  How far does it travel before decaying, as measured from Earth?

  <details>
    <summary>Show solution</summary>

  Compute $\gamma$:
  $$\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 - 0.995^2}} \approx 10.0$$

  Earth-frame lifetime: $\Delta t = \gamma\tau = 22\,\mu\text{s}$.
  Distance: $d = v\,\Delta t = 0.995 \times 3\times10^8 \times 22\times10^{-6} \approx 6.6\,\text{km}$.

  </details>
</div>
